
Case Study: Website Evaluation Before Development

Objective:
Before developing a website for a client, it is important to evaluate existing websites to understand design best practices, usability, content organization, and user experience. This case study reviews five different websites and analyzes their strengths, weaknesses, and overall effectiveness.

---

  Website 1: Amazon

URL: [https://www.amazon.in](https://www.amazon.in)
Purpose of Website: E‑commerce platform for online shopping
Things Liked:

* Clear categories and search functionality
* Quick product filtering and sorting
* Detailed product descriptions, reviews, and ratings
* Easy checkout and secure payment process
  Things Disliked:
* Homepage feels cluttered due to promotional banners
* Too many pop‑ups and advertisements
  Overall Evaluation: Good

---

  Website 2: Zomato

URL: [https://www.zomato.com](https://www.zomato.com)
Purpose of Website: Food ordering and restaurant discovery
Things Liked:

* Clean interface with attractive visuals
* Smooth filtering and location‑based search
* High‑quality restaurant images
* Real‑time order tracking
  Things Disliked:
* Heavy graphics slow down loading on low‑speed internet
* Occasional pop‑ups can interrupt user experience
  Overall Evaluation: Good

---

  Website 3: Wikipedia

URL: [https://www.wikipedia.org](https://www.wikipedia.org)
Purpose of Website: Free online encyclopedia for information
Things Liked:

* Extremely fast loading speed
* Simple and minimalistic design
* Easy navigation between articles
* Reliable, well‑structured content
  Things Disliked:
* Design is very plain, lacks visual appeal
* No interactive elements or multimedia
  Overall Evaluation: Good

---

  Website 4: Local College Website

URL: [https://www.examplecollege.edu](https://www.examplecollege.edu)
Purpose of Website: Providing information about courses, admissions, and faculty
Things Liked:

* Important academic information available
* Contact and location details provided
  Things Disliked:
* Outdated content
* Poorly organized menu and links
* Not mobile‑friendly, difficult to read on small screens
  Overall Evaluation: Bad

---

  Website 5: Old E‑commerce Website

URL: [https://www.exampleoldshop.com](https://www.exampleoldshop.com)
Purpose of Website: Online product selling
Things Liked:

* Product list available
  Things Disliked:
* Confusing layout and navigation
* Slow page loading
* Inconsistent fonts and colors
* Poor checkout process
  Overall Evaluation: Bad

---

  Observations from Evaluation

• Navigation Matters: Websites with clear menus, search functionality, and logical content grouping provide a better user experience. Confusing menus or lack of navigation frustrate users.
• Visual Design: Attractive visuals, consistent color schemes, and spacing improve engagement; plain or inconsistent designs may seem dull or unprofessional.
• Performance and Speed: Fast‑loading pages retain users; heavy graphics or slow servers reduce usability.
• Mobile Responsiveness: Modern websites need to work on mobile; non‑responsive designs are a major drawback.
• Content Quality: Up‑to‑date, accurate, and well‑organized content improves trust; outdated content diminishes credibility.
• User Interaction & Feedback: Interactive elements like ratings, reviews, order tracking enhance user engagement; lack of feedback mechanisms limits experience.
• Advertisements and Clutter: Excessive ads reduce clarity; minimal distractions help focus on main content.

---

  Key Website Design Issues Learned

1. Layout and Structure: Use clear sections, spacing, and alignment to make content easy to read.
2. Navigation: Ensure menus are intuitive; include search and filters if content is extensive.
3. Responsiveness: Websites must work seamlessly across devices.
4. Performance: Optimize images, scripts, and server response to improve speed.
5. Consistency: Maintain consistent color schemes, fonts, and button styles across pages.
6. Content Quality: Always provide accurate, up‑to‑date information.
7. Interactivity: Include user‑friendly features like forms, reviews, and feedback mechanisms.
8. Minimal Clutter: Avoid unnecessary ads, pop‑ups, and heavy graphics that distract users.
9. Accessibility: Ensure text readability, alt texts for images, and good contrast for users with disabilities.
10. Security & Trust: Secure transactions and visible privacy policies are critical for e‑commerce sites.

---

  Conclusion

Evaluating multiple websites before starting development provides valuable insights:

• Good websites (Amazon, Zomato, Wikipedia) prioritize user experience, clarity, and reliability.
• Bad websites (local college, old e‑commerce) often fail due to poor navigation, slow loading, and outdated content.
• For successful website development, the planning phase should consider layout, performance, mobile responsiveness, content quality, interactivity, and aesthetic design.

By studying both good and bad examples, developers can avoid common mistakes and implement best practices to create efficient, professional, and user‑friendly websites.
